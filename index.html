Political Polarization and Bridging on Facebook (work in progress)

Research work in Social Dynamics Lab
Collaborators: Alex Ruch, Hannah Lee

Abstract:
The rapid growth of publicly available social media data has facilitated new approaches of study for computational social scientists, allowing researchers to examine human behavior from their digital traces at an unprecedented scale. We constructed a unique dataset of 300,000 Facebook interests to analyze political polarization among aggregate groups of users to better understand the political partisanship of different lifestyles (e.g., enjoying lattes vs. hunting). We classified the most and least polarized classes of topics and identified specific topics that serve as cultural bridges on which opposing groups agree within larger contexts of disagreement.

Testing the “Conjunction Fallacy” Using AMT Workers

Completed for CS 5306: Crowdsourcing & Human Computation
Collaborators: Alex Cho

Our experiment tests the bias of “conjunction fallacy,” which occurs when people falsely assume that specific conditions are more probable than a single general condition. The most famous test of this cognitive bias was conducted by Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman. They couple a passage about a fictitious woman named Linda with a question asking which is more probable: “Linda is a bank teller” or “Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.” Most people choose the second option, even though the probability of two events occurring together (in conjunction) is always less than or equal to the probability of one of the events occurring on its own, i.e. P(A∩B) <= P(A) and P(A∩B) <= P(B).
